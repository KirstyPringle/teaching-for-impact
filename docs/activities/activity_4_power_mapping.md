## Why people are central to advocacy

No matter how strong your evidence, how clear your argument, or how well-designed your plan, change happens through people. Understanding who those people are, what they care about, and how they relate to each other is as important as understanding the issue itself.

This section gives you tools to analyse the human landscape of your organisation — who holds power, who influences decisions, and how to use that knowledge to shape your strategy.

---

<!-- type: concept -->

## Power – what it is and who has it

In most organisations, formal power structures — org charts, job titles, committees — tell only part of the story. Decisions are shaped by relationships, incentives, and informal influence as much as by official authority.

One useful lens for thinking about this is **selectorate theory**.

---

<!-- type: framework -->
## Selectorate Theory

- Most research organizations aren't democracies
  - Faculty and students don't elect the university president

- Selectorate theory explores how power structures work:
  - **Nominal selectorate**: those who have the right to have a say
  - **Real selectorate**: those who actually cast a vote
  - **Winning coalition**: those whose votes produce victory

- Key insights:
  1. The smaller the winning coalition, the fewer people the person in charge needs to satisfy to remain in control.
  2. The larger the selectorate, the easier it is to replace dissenters.
  3. Extract as much as you can without provoking rebellion or recession.
  4. Give your essential supporters just enough rewards to keep them loyal.
  5. Do not reward them too well or they will become a threat.

## Power mapping

Power mapping is a practical tool for visualising where power sits in relation to your cause. It helps you move from a general understanding of how institutions work to a specific picture of your own situation.

A good power mapping process asks:

- Who has the formal authority to approve or block the change you want?
- Who influences those people, even without formal authority?
- Who is affected by the issue, and do they have any leverage?
- Who are your potential allies, and who might actively resist?

For a detailed guide to power mapping, the [Museum of Protest](https://museumofprotest.org/guides/guide-power-analysis-and-stakeholder-mapping/) has an excellent practical resource.

---

## Stakeholder analysis

Once you have a picture of where power sits, you can start to categorise the specific people involved. A simple and effective starting point is to classify stakeholders as:

- **Allies** — broadly supportive of your cause; potential collaborators
- **Opponents** — likely to resist or actively work against change
- **Neutrals** — not yet engaged; could be moved in either direction

The goal of stakeholder analysis is not just to identify who is who, but to understand *why* — what are their interests, incentives, and constraints? This shapes how you approach each person.

## Worked Example: Power Mapping at Greendale University

Greendale is a fictional mid-sized research-intensive university in the UK. A small group of researchers and RSEs want to advocate for a policy requiring all research computing to be reported as part of the university's carbon footprint.

The following people are relevant to whether this change happens:

| Person | Role | Interest in the issue | Formal power | Informal influence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Prof. Sarah Okafor** | Vice-Chancellor | Low — focused on rankings and income | Very high — ultimate decision-maker | High — sets the institutional tone |
| **Dr. James Whitfield** | Head of Research Computing | Medium — aware of the issue, cautious about extra workload | High — controls relevant infrastructure and data | Medium — respected technically |
| **Dr. Priya Nair** | Sustainability Manager | Very high — this is her agenda | Low — advisory role only | Medium — knows the policy landscape well |
| **Prof. Duncan Laird** | Dean of Research | Medium — supportive in principle, risk-averse in practice | High — signs off research policy | Medium — influential with heads of department |
| **Ravi Okonkwo** | RSE, informal community organiser | Very high — one of the advocates | Low — no formal authority | High — trusted and well-connected across departments |
| **Dr. Hannah Frost** | Head of Finance | Low — focused on cost efficiency | Medium — any policy with resource implications crosses her desk | Medium — can quietly block or enable things |

## Personas of key power players

A power map tells you who matters. A persona helps you think carefully about how to approach them.

A persona is a brief, structured description of a person — their role, motivations, constraints, and likely response to your advocacy. Personas are useful because they force you to move beyond categories ("a risk-averse senior manager") and think concretely about a specific individual's perspective.

A useful persona for advocacy purposes includes:

- **Role and context** — what do they do, and what pressures are they under?
- **What they care about** — what are their priorities and incentives?
- **Their likely response** — are they an ally, opponent, or neutral? What might shift them?
- **How to reach them** — what messages, framing, or relationships are most likely to be effective?

!!! tip

	You don't need to know someone well to write a useful persona. Even a rough sketch based on their role and public behaviour is better than treating them as an abstraction. And the process of writing the persona often reveals gaps in what you know — which is itself useful.

## Using your analysis to decide who to target and how

Power mapping, stakeholder analysis, and personas combine into a targeting strategy: a considered plan for who to approach, in what order, and with what message.

Some principles to guide this:

- **Start with allies, not decision-makers.** Build your coalition before you approach the people with formal power. Arriving with support is more persuasive than arriving alone.
- **Don't neglect neutrals.** Opponents are often less important than they appear; neutrals who could become allies are frequently more valuable to win over.
- **Match your message to the person.** The case you make to a sustainability manager is different from the case you make to a head of finance. Both need to hear why this matters — but what "matters" means is different for each of them.
- **Informal influence is often more important than formal authority.** A trusted peer who endorses your idea can open doors that a formal proposal cannot.
