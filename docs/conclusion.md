# Conclusion

This course has taken you through the full advocacy cycle:

- from **understanding the issue** — the policies, commitments, and gaps that give you a foundation
- to **mapping people and power** — who has influence, who needs to be convinced, and how to reach them
- to **developing a plan** — connecting cause, goal, strategy, and tactics through a clear theory of change
- to **implementing that plan** — building coalitions, navigating politics, and closing the deal
- to **following up and evaluating** — holding commitments to account and learning from what happened

These steps are not a checklist. They are a cycle. Each time you go around it, you know more than you did before.

---

### Key messages to carry forward

- **Change happens through people**
  Evidence matters, but decisions are made by human beings with priorities, pressures, and relationships. Understanding the people is as important as understanding the issue.

- **Politics is not a dirty word**
  Forming a winning coalition is not cynical — it is how change gets made in organisations that do not agree on everything. Compassionate people who refuse to think strategically cede ground to those who don't share their values.

- **Start small and build**
  A modest early win is more valuable than an ambitious proposal that fails. It builds credibility, develops skills, and creates the community of people you will need for the next step.

- **Your institution's own commitments are your most powerful tool**
  When you can show that a change is consistent with what leadership has already publicly agreed to, you are asking them to follow through — not asking them to do something new.

- **Advocacy is sustained effort, not a single moment**
  Most meaningful institutional change takes longer than expected and involves more setbacks than anyone advertises. Pace yourself. Look after the people working alongside you.

- **The cycle is iterative**
  You will not get it right the first time. That is not failure — it is how advocacy works. Every round teaches you something the previous one could not.

---

### Your role going forward

You now have a framework, a set of tools, and — most importantly — a way of thinking about how change happens in organisations.

What matters next is action.

!!! tip
    Start where you are. Use what you have. Help who you can.

You might:

- apply the power mapping and stakeholder analysis to a change you are already thinking about
- have one honest conversation with someone who has tried something similar before
- identify one gap between your institution's stated commitments and current practice — and use it as a lever
- find one Connector or Guru and share your idea with them
- draft your theory of change in a single sentence and test it with a colleague

None of these require permission. All of them move something forward.

---

### Next steps

- **Return to the advocacy cycle**
  After any campaign — successful or not — go back to step one. The landscape will have changed, and so will you.

- **Build and maintain your network**
  The relationships you develop through advocacy are worth tending even when there is no immediate ask. Future campaigns are easier when they start from existing trust.

- **Share what you learn**
  The research and innovation community benefits when people share not just what worked, but what didn't, and why. Your experience is useful to others attempting similar things.

- **Stay connected**
  Advocacy is more sustainable in community than in isolation. Find others working on environmental sustainability in research — within your institution and beyond it.

---

!!! note
    Environmental sustainability in research is complex and there is no quick fix. Change is possible, but it requires sustained advocacy, learning, and returning to the cycle, over time and with others.
